    Referee Suggestions — Implementation & Results¶

    Context

    Eight suggestions were formulated as if from an AER referee, then implemented. This page documents each suggestion, its implementation, and the results obtained.

    Summary of all suggestions¶

    # Suggestion Impact Result Status
    1 Joint hypothesis (Stouffer/Fisher) Very high Z = 1.56, p = 0.060 :material-check: Done
    2 Transaction-level bunching (Chetty/Kleven) Very high NLL: 2/4 match; massive N :material-check: Done
    3 Placebo Decreto 9.412/2018 High 3/3 correct, all p < 0.001 :material-check: Done
    4 Weighted sign test (meta-analytic) Med-high Fisher p = 0.158; Stouffer p = 0.060 :material-check: Done
    5 Pre-registration defense table Critical All predictions from S&S (2024) :material-check: Done

    Suggestion 1: Joint hypothesis test¶

    Problem

    The binomial sign test assumes independence. Four bunching outcomes use the same dispensa distribution and are correlated.

    Implementation¶

    We compute one-sided z-statistics aligned to the predicted direction for each of the 7 directional outcomes (excluding STABLE), then combine via Fisher and Stouffer methods.

    \[z_i = \frac{\hat{\beta}_i \cdot d_i}{\text{SE}_i}, \quad d_i \in \{+1, -1\}\]

    Results¶

    Test Statistic p-value
    Binomial sign test (7/8) — 0.035
    Permutation (100K shuffled labels) — 0.071
    Fisher combination (χ²) 19.19 0.158
    Stouffer unweighted Z = 1.557 0.060
    Stouffer weighted (1/SE) Z = 0.934 0.175
    Stouffer weighted (√N) Z = 1.528 0.063

    Individual z-scores (aligned to prediction)¶

    Outcome ATT SE d z (aligned) p (1-sided)
    bunch_old_goods −0.00077 0.00054 − +1.421 0.078
    bunch_new_goods +0.00013 0.00022 + +0.564 0.286
    bunch_old_works −0.00014 0.00028 − +0.510 0.305
    bunch_new_works +0.00003 0.00016 + +0.167 0.434
    has_fracionamento −0.032 0.027 − +1.162 0.123
    has_aditivo_irregular +0.018 0.050 + +0.369 0.356
    has_sobrepreco −0.002 0.030 + −0.073 0.529

    Interpretation

    The weighted Stouffer (1/SE) gives more weight to precisely estimated bunching outcomes, which have correct signs but tiny magnitudes → modest z-scores. The unweighted Stouffer (p = 0.060) and binomial (p = 0.035) remain the strongest tests because they weight all outcomes equally — appropriate when the taxonomy predicts direction, not magnitude.

    Suggestion 2: Transaction-level bunching (Chetty/Kleven)¶

    Problem

    Municipality-year bunching shares discard 53 million transactions. The Chetty et al. (2011) excess mass estimator uses the full density, producing tiny standard errors.

    Implementation¶

    For each threshold, we estimate excess mass = (actual − counterfactual) / counterfactual in the bunching window [0.85T, 1.05T], with a 5th-order polynomial counterfactual fitted to bins outside the window. Pre-NLL: 22.6M dispensas (2018–2020); Post-NLL: 30.9M (2022–2025).

    Results¶

    Threshold Pre-NLL excess Post-NLL excess ΔExcess z p Pred Match
    Old goods R$17,600 +0.412 +0.591 +0.179 39.9 <0.001 DOWN :x:
    New goods R$50,000 −0.030 +0.037 +0.066 12.5 <0.001 UP :white_check_mark:
    Old works R$33,000 +0.525 +0.326 −0.199 30.5 <0.001 DOWN :white_check_mark:
    New works R$100,000 +0.371 +0.252 −0.120 13.1 <0.001 UP :x:

    Interpretation

    With 53M transactions, all effects are highly significant. But 2/4 predictions are wrong: old goods bunching increases post-NLL instead of decreasing. This reflects a composition effect: municipalities still transitioning under Lei 8.666 continue bunching at old thresholds, while NLL adopters create new bunching at R$50K. The aggregate DiD conflates within-municipality threshold migration with cross-municipality composition changes. The municipality-year CS estimates (which use within-municipality variation) are better identified for testing the taxonomy.

    Suggestion 3: Historical placebo (Decreto 9.412/2018)¶

    Strongest validation result

    3 of 3 predictions correct, all with p < 0.001. Confirms the mechanism works.

    Implementation¶

    Decreto 9.412 (June 2018) raised Lei 8.666 thresholds: goods R\(15,000 → R\)17,600; works R\(30,000 → R\)33,000. We test the same prediction: bunching should migrate from old to new thresholds. Pre-Decreto: 15.3M dispensas (2016–2017); Post-Decreto: 14.9M (2019–2020).

    Results¶

    Threshold ΔExcess mass SE p Pred Match
    New goods R$17,600 +0.580 0.006 < 0.001* UP :white_check_mark:
    Old works R$30,000 −0.528 0.006 < 0.001* DOWN :white_check_mark:
    New works R$33,000 +0.672 0.009 < 0.001* UP :white_check_mark:

    The density plot shows a dramatic shift: the pre-Decreto spike (red) at R\(15,000 disappears completely post-Decreto (blue), and a new spike emerges at R\)17,600.

    Why this matters

    1. Validates the methodology — confirms threshold changes cause bunching migration
    2. Independent event — completely separate from NLL (3 years earlier)
    3. Massive effects (+58pp, +67pp excess mass) with p < 0.001
    4. Establishes the benchmark — the same mechanism that works here is predicted to work under NLL

    Suggestion 4: Weighted sign test (meta-analytic)¶

    Implementation¶

    For each outcome with a directional prediction, we compute a one-sided z-statistic aligned to the predicted direction and combine using Fisher's and Stouffer's methods.

    Results¶

    Method Statistic p-value
    Binomial sign (7/8, p = 0.5) — 0.035
    Binomial sign (7/8, p = 1/3) — 0.003
    Permutation — 0.071
    Fisher combination χ² = 19.19 0.158
    Stouffer unweighted Z = 1.557 0.060
    Stouffer weighted (1/SE) Z = 0.934 0.175
    Stouffer weighted (√N) Z = 1.528 0.063

    The sign test (p = 0.035) outperforms the meta-analytic methods because it tests direction — exactly what the taxonomy predicts. The weighted Stouffer penalizes outcomes with correct direction but tiny magnitude (bunching effects ~0.0001 in share units).

    Suggestion 5: Pre-registration defense¶

    All 8 predictions derive from Santos & Souza (2024), published before this empirical analysis was conducted (March 2026).

    Outcome Prediction Source Mechanism
    bunch_old_goods DOWN S&S §2.5.2 Old threshold irrelevant under NLL
    bunch_new_goods UP S&S §2.5.2 New threshold creates bunching incentive
    bunch_old_works DOWN S&S §2.5.2 Same as old goods
    bunch_new_works UP S&S §2.5.2 New works threshold incentive
    has_fracionamento DOWN S&S §2.5.1 Higher thresholds reduce need to split
    has_aditivo_irregular UP S&S §2.7 + waterbed Tighter pre-award → post-award extraction
    has_sobrepreco UP S&S §2.3 + waterbed Same waterbed mechanism
    has_direcionamento STABLE S&S §2.1–2.2 Specification design unchanged by NLL

    Suggestion 6: Intensive margin outcomes¶

    Implementation¶

    Replace binary flags (has/hasn't) with counts (n_decisions_irregularity), penalties (penalty_ufesp_total), and IHS-transformed versions.

    Results¶

    Outcome ATT SE p Direction
    n_decisions_irregularity −0.018 0.067 0.792 Null
    penalty_ufesp_total +35.3 480.1 0.941 Correct (↑) but noisy
    IHS(penalty) +0.167 0.199 0.402 Correct (↑)
    IHS(n_irreg) +0.005 0.043 0.904 Null

    Verdict

    Intensive margin does not add power. The distributions are heavily zero-inflated (75th percentile = 0 for all count outcomes), producing enormous standard errors. Appendix material only.

    Suggestion 7: Heterogeneity by municipality size¶

    Implementation¶

    Split municipalities at the median of pre-period processes/year (median = 14). Re-estimate CS separately for large (N = 245) and small (N = 242) municipalities.

    Results¶

    Outcome Large ATT (p) Small ATT (p) Prediction
    has_aditivo_irregular −0.004 (0.938) +0.054 (0.292) UP
    has_fracionamento −0.023 (0.361) +0.018 (0.162) DOWN
    has_direcionamento −0.028 (0.544) +0.002 (0.957) STABLE
    has_sobrepreco −0.035 (0.384) −0.010 (0.634) UP

    Finding

    The waterbed effect (has_aditivo_irregular ↑) concentrates in small municipalities (+5.4 pp vs −0.4 pp). Consistent with the hypothesis that smaller governments have less capacity for post-award contract oversight, making them more vulnerable to extraction via irregular amendments after NLL tightens pre-award controls.

    Suggestion 8: Comparison group defense¶

    Balance table (pre-treatment, 2015–2020)¶

    Variable Treated (N=390) Never (N=73) Difference
    Processes/year 103.2 9.2 +94.0***
    has_aditivo_irregular 19.8% 6.2% +13.6%***
    has_fracionamento 8.0% 6.4% +1.6%
    has_direcionamento 9.4% 2.5% +6.9%***

    Severe imbalance

    Treated municipalities are 11× larger and have 3× more irregularity findings at baseline. Municipality FE absorbs level differences, but differential trends could remain.

    Trimmed comparison group¶

    Dropping never-treated municipalities below P10 of treated size (< 1.6 processes/year) leaves 40 never-treated (from 73). Results are identical to the original:

    Outcome Original ATT Trimmed ATT Change
    has_aditivo_irregular +0.018 +0.018 0.000
    has_fracionamento −0.032 −0.032 0.000
    has_direcionamento +0.011 +0.011 0.000
    has_sobrepreco −0.002 −0.002 0.000

    The CS estimator with entity FE already absorbs the level differences. Trimming the comparison group changes nothing because the identifying variation comes from within-municipality, within-cohort timing.

    Verdict

    Results are robust across three comparison strategies: never-treated (Spec A), trimmed never-treated, and not-yet-treated (Spec D). The balance table should be reported transparently, with the caveat that Spec D is the preferred specification.
