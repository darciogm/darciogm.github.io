## Abstract

Judicial enforcement is essential for the rule of law, yet the *form* of enforcement can generate economic inefficiency. We study this tension in public procurement, where courts compel governments to purchase pharmaceuticals under tight deadlines and officials face personal sanctions---fines, liability, fund seizure---if tenders fail. Using bid-level data from Sao Paulo, Brazil (2009--2019) with item, time, and buyer fixed effects, court-mandated purchases raise prices by 5.4% and reduce bidder participation by 5.5%. We exploit an institutional distinction between two types of urgent purchases that share planning constraints but differ in penalty exposure to isolate the "under the gun" effect: the threat of judicial punishment alone raises procurement costs by 23--30%, operating entirely through demand fragmentation---officials cannot aggregate orders---rather than through direct price inflation. The mechanism is general: whenever accountability regimes penalize non-completion more heavily than overpayment, procurement officials will prioritize compliance over cost-efficiency.

## Key Findings

!!! success "Judicial mandates raise procurement costs"
    Court-ordered purchases increase **reference prices by 2.7%** and **negotiated prices by 5.4%** compared to ordinary procurement, after controlling for item, year, and buyer fixed effects.

!!! warning "Competition declines under judicial pressure"
    Litigated purchases attract **5.5% fewer bidding firms**, reducing competitive pressure and contributing to higher prices.

!!! danger "The 'under the gun' effect is substantial"
    When comparing litigated purchases to administrative urgent purchases---which share the same urgency but lack judicial sanctions---the **price premium is 23--30%**. This isolates the pure effect of judicial enforcement pressure on procurement officials.

!!! info "Officials accept worse terms to comply"
    Tender success rates are **2.1 percentage points higher** for litigated purchases, indicating that procurement officials accept less favorable bids to meet court deadlines.

!!! example "Half supply-side, half demand-side"
    Supplier fixed effects (2,202 firms) attenuate the urgency coefficient by **52%** (from 0.051 to 0.025), revealing that roughly half the premium reflects firms charging more when the purchase is court-mandated, and half reflects officials selecting into worse matches under time pressure. A placebo test on items never subject to litigation confirms the premium is **absent where judicial pressure plays no role** (coefficient $-0.020$, n.s.).
