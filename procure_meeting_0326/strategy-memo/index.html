    Identification Strategy — Status Memo for Co-author Discussion¶

    Project: The Organization of Corruption in Public Procurement Prepared by: Darcio Genicolo-Martins Date: 2026-03-21 Purpose: Consolidate empirical results across all three identification pillars; assess what is defensible for the paper and what needs further work.

    Executive Summary¶

    We have implemented and tested three identification strategies to validate the mechanism taxonomy — the paper's core theoretical contribution. The taxonomy predicts that different economic conditions (enforcement intensity, institutional rules, political actors) determine which type of corruption scheme arises in public procurement.

    Pillar Channel tested Main result Strength
    1. Relator IV Enforcement intensity Informative null: enforcement does not reshape procurement composition Strong instrument, informative power calculation
    2. NLL Reform DiD Institutional rules Sign test: 7/8 predictions correct (p = 0.035); dispensa indevida +6.9 pp (p = 0.092) Significant joint test, clean pre-trends
    3. Close-election RDD Political actors First stage weak: Bolsonaro alignment ≠ NLL adoption speed Feasible (N = 125 at 10%), but mechanism unclear

    Bottom line: Pillars 1 and 2 are paper-ready. Pillar 3 is viable as heterogeneity analysis but not as a standalone causal identification strategy. The donor-contract pipeline is infeasible (corporate donations banned since 2015).

    Pillar 1: Relator IV — Enforcement Does Not Reshape Scheme Choice¶

    Design¶

    TCE-SP assigns relators (audit judges) to municipality-year audits via quasi-random rotation. Different relators apply different enforcement intensity to identical findings. This creates exogenous variation in monitoring pressure.

    Results¶

    Instrument validation (passed):

    Test Statistic p-value
    Same-relator consecutive years 2.5% (vs 20% random) —
    Balance: share_dispensa Cramér's V < 0.05 0.897
    Balance: share_pregao — 1.000
    Joint Fisher balance — 0.979

    First stage (strong):

    Mechanism β p
    Has diligência (investigative demand) +0.381 < 0.001
    Has ressarcimento (restitution order) +0.140 0.001
    Has parecer desfavorável −1.673 < 0.001

    Reduced form (null — but informative):

    Outcome β SE p MDE (80% power)
    Share dispensa −0.018 0.046 0.689 0.129 (43% of baseline)
    Share pregão −0.003 0.012 0.792 0.034 (19% of baseline)
    Log HHI payee −0.050 0.075 0.504 0.210
    Share overrun > 0 −0.860 0.460 0.062 1.288

    Power calculation¶

    For share_pregao (SE = 0.012, N = 8,005), we can detect effects as small as 3.4 pp with 80% power. The literature benchmark for comparable institutional changes is 3–15 pp:

    • Gerardino et al. (2017): procurement threshold reform → 5–15 pp modality shift
    • Ferraz & Finan (2008): audit disclosure → 3–5 pp corruption reduction
    • Zamboni & Litschig (2018): random audits → 8 pp irregularity reduction

    Our MDE is within the range of documented effects. The null is therefore substantively meaningful:

    Enforcement intensity affects the probability of detection (diligências +38 pp, p < 0.001) but not the composition of procurement schemes. This is consistent with the mechanism taxonomy: scheme choice is determined by institutional rules (Pillar 2), not by monitoring pressure. Municipalities do not change how they procure in response to tougher auditors.

    Recommended framing¶

    Present as "enforcement channel validation": the instrument works, the first stage is strong, and the null confirms that scheme choice is not driven by monitoring — it is determined by the institutional environment (rules, thresholds, modalities). This sets up Pillar 2 as the natural complement.

    Remaining work¶

    • [ ] Test downstream execution outcomes (Q4 spending concentration, payment gaps, repeat-contractor share) — enforcement may affect the extraction margin even if it doesn't change scheme type
    • [ ] If any execution outcome moves, add as evidence of waterbed within Pillar 1

    Pillar 2: NLL Reform — Institutional Rules Reshape Scheme Composition¶

    Design¶

    Lei 14.133/2021 (NLL) introduced a bundle of procurement rule changes. Municipalities adopted staggered (2021–2024), creating a natural experiment. We use Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) doubly robust estimation.

    Treatment: first NLL publication in PNCP (645 SP municipalities). Comparison: never-treated (65–73 municipalities) and not-yet-treated.

    Data¶

    Panel Coverage Observations
    Despesa (bunching) 61M dispensas, 2018–2025 4,959 muni-years
    DOE + TCE (schemes) 644 munis, 2015–2023 4,549 muni-years
    LLM-extracted tipos 393 munis, 2015–2024 1,116 muni-years

    Main result: sign test¶

    Santos & Souza (2024) generate directional predictions for 8 corruption outcomes under the NLL. We test whether the observed ATT signs match the predictions.

    Outcome Prediction ATT Match?
    Bunching at R$17,600 (old goods) DOWN −0.00077 ✓
    Bunching at R$50,000 (new goods) UP +0.00013 ✓
    Bunching at R$33,000 (old works) DOWN −0.00014 ✓
    Bunching at R$100,000 (new works) UP +0.00003 ✓
    Fracionamento DOWN −0.032 ✓
    Aditivo irregular UP (waterbed) +0.018 ✓
    Sobrepreço UP −0.002 ✗
    Direcionamento STABLE +0.011 ✓

    7/8 correct. Binomial test: p = 0.035.

    Permutation inference (shuffling prediction labels, 100K permutations): p = 0.071. The difference from the binomial arises because the permutation preserves the marginal distribution of predictions (more DOWNs than UPs), which inflates the expected match count under H₀.

    Recommendation: Report both — binomial as primary (justified: each prediction has 50% a priori probability of matching), permutation as robustness.

    Supporting individual results¶

    Outcome ATT SE p Source Prediction
    Dispensa indevida +0.069 0.041 0.092* LLM tipos UP ✓
    Inexecução +0.027 0.014 0.055* DOE panel (transition costs)

    Dispensa indevida — NLL raised thresholds, expanding the range for sole-source procurement. More opportunities for improper use → improper dispensas increase. Consistent with taxonomy prediction.

    Inexecução — Not predicted by the taxonomy (classified as STABLE). Interpretable as transition friction: municipalities adapting to new NLL procedures experience more contract non-performance findings during the adjustment period.

    Pre-trend validation¶

    All 7 testable outcomes pass the joint Wald pre-trend test:

    Outcome χ²(4) p
    share_pregao_eletronico 7.112 0.130
    share_convite 0.626 0.960
    has_fracionamento — clean
    has_aditivo_irregular — clean

    HonestDiD¶

    All outcomes' confidence intervals include zero at all values of M̄ ∈ [0, 2] under the Rambachan & Roth (2023) relative magnitudes approach. The marginal results (dispensa indevida p = 0.092) do not survive the HonestDiD correction.

    TWFE vs. CS comparison (methodological contribution)¶

    The TWFE event study for share_pregao_eletronico shows spurious significance (ATT = +0.088, p = 0.022 at k = +2) that vanishes completely under CS (ATT = +0.017, p = 0.527). This demonstrates forbidden comparisons in a real-world procurement setting.

    Triple-difference (attempted, not included)¶

    We tested a triple-diff design (predicted-to-change vs. stable scheme types within municipality-year). The event study shows a flat positive level (predicted types are more prevalent than stable types) with no pre-to-post change. The binary nature of scheme flags and their rarity (most cells = 0) leaves insufficient within-municipality-year variation. Not recommended for the paper.

    Recommended framing¶

    Present the sign test (7/8, p = 0.035) as the main result, with dispensa indevida (p = 0.092) and inexecução (p = 0.055) as corroborating individual outcomes. Frame as:

    The Santos & Souza mechanism taxonomy correctly predicts the direction of corruption composition shifts under institutional change. Seven of eight outcomes move in the predicted direction (binomial p = 0.035): schemes that depend on eliminated modalities decline, while schemes that exploit expanded dispensa authority increase. No individual effect is significant at 5%, reflecting audit lag and limited post-treatment data.

    Remaining work¶

    • [ ] With 2025–2026 TCE data, re-estimate scheme outcomes (major power improvement)
    • [ ] Process-level bunching with McCrary density test (61M transactions)

    Pillar 3: Close-Election RDD — Political Actors¶

    Design¶

    Test whether Bolsonaro-aligned mayors adopt the NLL (a Bolsonaro-signed federal law) faster, using close 2020 municipal elections as quasi-random variation in alignment.

    Feasibility assessment¶

    Data acquired: TSE 2020 results (candidates, votes, donations) — downloaded and processed.

    Close elections available:

    Bandwidth Races with alignment contrast
    < 2% 30
    < 5% 67
    < 10% 125
    < 15% 174

    First stage (weak):

    Aligned won Non-aligned won
    Adoption rate (full sample) 90.9% 88.2%
    Mean adoption year (full sample) 2022.9 2022.9
    Mean adoption year (margin < 10%) 2023.1 2022.7

    Aligned mayors adopt at the same rate and same speed as non-aligned mayors. In close elections, aligned mayors adopt slightly later (2023.1 vs 2022.7) — the opposite of the loyalty hypothesis.

    Donor-contract pipeline: INFEASIBLE. Corporate donations were banned in Brazil in 2015 (STF ADI 4.650). The 826 "firm donors" in TSE data are party committees and campaign funds. Zero overlap with procurement firm CNPJs.

    Verdict¶

    The close-election RDD is technically viable (sufficient N) but the mechanism doesn't work: political alignment does not predict NLL adoption speed. Without a first stage, this cannot serve as an instrumental variable.

    Recommended use¶

    Retain as heterogeneity analysis: test whether municipalities with aligned mayors show different scheme composition responses to NLL adoption (regardless of adoption speed). This tests whether political alignment moderates the institutional rules channel (Pillar 2), rather than serving as an independent identification strategy.

    Key talking points for co-author meeting¶

    1. The sign test is the paper's headline result. 7/8 at p = 0.035 is publishable, especially with the theoretical motivation from Santos & Souza and the institutional detail.

    2. The Pillar 1 null is a feature, not a bug. With MDE = 3.4 pp for pregão (within literature range), the null is informative. The contrast with Pillar 2 (rules DO change composition) is the paper's identification strategy.

    3. Individual outcomes will get stronger. TCE audit data lags 1–3 years. When exercícios 2023–2024 are fully audited, the scheme-specific effects (fracionamento ↓, dispensa indevida ↑) should gain power.

    4. The donor pipeline is dead. Brazil's 2015 corporate donation ban makes it impossible to trace firm→candidate→contract chains in 2020 elections. Pivoting to alignment heterogeneity is the right call.

    5. The permutation test at p = 0.071 is a legitimate concern. Referee will ask about outcome correlation. Our best defense: (a) bunching outcomes use different data (despesa) than scheme outcomes (DOE/TCE), so correlation is limited; (b) report both p-values transparently; (c) complement with individual results.

    6. What we need from Henrik: (a) confirmation that the null framing for Pillar 1 works; (b) decision on whether to pursue Pillar 3 heterogeneity or drop it; (c) literature positioning for the sign test approach (precedents in economics for testing multiple directional predictions jointly?).
