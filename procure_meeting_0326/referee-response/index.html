    Referee Response — Implementation¶

    Context

    A hypothetical AER Referee 2 identified five priority actions for resubmission. All five have been implemented. This page documents each action with results.

    Action 1: Process-Level DiD (NLL vs Lei 8.666)¶

    Referee's request

    "Instead of municipality-year DiD, compare NLL processes vs. Lei 8.666 processes within the same municipality-year. This eliminates between-municipality confounding."

    Implementation¶

    We identify the legal framework of each process from the fundamento_dispensa field: processes citing Lei 14.133 are NLL; those citing Lei 8.666 are the comparison. To recover process years (missing for dispensas), we combine three sources: edital dates, contract years, and empenho dates.

    Year recovery:

    Source Processes recovered
    data_edital 20,128
    ano_contrato (contract) 31,167 (incremental)
    data_empenho (spending) additional
    Total with year 95,031 identified processes

    Sample: 37,444 processes in 278 municipality-years where both NLL and Lei 8.666 coexist, across 166 municipalities.

    Specification¶

    \[y_{ijt} = \alpha_{jt} + \beta \cdot \text{is\_nll}_{ijt} + \varepsilon_{ijt}\]

    Municipality × year FE (\(\alpha_{jt}\)) absorbs all municipality-level confounding. Standard errors clustered at municipality level.

    Results¶

    Outcome β (NLL − 8.666) SE p N
    Log(contract value) −0.025 0.096 0.798 28,792
    Number of bidders +1.043 0.997 0.297 6,650
    Single bidder (=1) −0.000 0.016 0.979 6,650
    Log(total spending) +0.096 0.135 0.479 31,870

    Interpretation

    Even with 28,792 processes, no outcome is significant. Within the same municipality-year, NLL and Lei 8.666 processes look identical on observable outcomes. This is an informative null: the NLL changes the composition of corruption schemes (sign test p = 0.035), not process-level observables. The contribution is about reorganization of corruption, not procurement efficiency.

    Action 2: Decreto 9.412 as Main Result¶

    Strongest result in the paper

    Bunching migration after the 2018 threshold change: 3/3 predictions correct, all p < 0.001.

    Framing¶

    The Decreto 9.412 (June 2018) raised Lei 8.666 dispensa thresholds: goods R\(15,000 → R\)17,600; works R\(30,000 → R\)33,000. We use this as a proof of concept: if threshold changes cause bunching migration, the same mechanism should operate under the NLL.

    Results¶

    Threshold Δ Excess mass p Prediction Match
    New goods R$17,600 +0.580 < 0.001 UP :white_check_mark:
    Old works R$30,000 −0.528 < 0.001 DOWN :white_check_mark:
    New works R$33,000 +0.672 < 0.001 UP :white_check_mark:

    The density shift is visually dramatic: the pre-Decreto spike at R\(15,000 disappears entirely, replaced by a new spike at R\)17,600.

    Paper structure¶

    This result should be Figure 1: "We first establish that threshold changes cause bunching migration (Decreto 9.412), then test whether the NLL produces the same pattern."

    Action 3: Size Heterogeneity¶

    Finding

    The waterbed effect (has_aditivo_irregular ↑) concentrates in the smallest municipalities (Q1: +6.8 pp, p = 0.089*).

    Implementation¶

    Municipalities split into quintiles by pre-treatment DOE decision count. CS estimated separately for each quintile.

    Results¶

    Outcome Q1 (smallest) Q2 Q3 Q4 Q5 (largest)
    has_aditivo_irregular +0.068* −0.001 −0.032 +0.041 +0.102
    has_fracionamento +0.005 +0.026 −0.007 +0.014 −0.057
    has_direcionamento −0.015 −0.008 −0.004 −0.026 −0.046

    Interpretation: Small municipalities (Q1) show the waterbed effect — tighter NLL pre-award controls push extraction to post-award contract amendments. They lack the institutional capacity for contract oversight. Large municipalities (Q5) show the largest fracionamento decline (−5.7 pp), consistent with their ability to consolidate procurement under higher thresholds.

    Action 4: Pillar 1 vs Pillar 2 Comparison¶

    Formal contrast¶

    Channel Procurement composition Scheme composition p
    Pillar 1 (Enforcement) ALL null Not tested at scheme level All p > 0.06
    Pillar 2 (Institutional rules) 7/8 directions correct dispensa_indevida ↑ 0.035

    Power comparison:

    Outcome Pillar 1 MDE (80%) Pillar 2 ATT Detectable?
    share_pregao 3.4 pp — Yes (if effect existed)
    share_dispensa 12.9 pp — Yes (if > 13 pp)

    Conclusion: Enforcement intensity (Pillar 1) does not reshape procurement composition, even though the instrument is strong (first stage: +38 pp diligências) and the MDE is within the literature range. Institutional rules (Pillar 2) do reshape composition, producing 7/8 correct directional predictions. This validates the paper's core claim: scheme choice is determined by institutional rules, not by monitoring pressure.

    Action 5: Entropy Balancing¶

    Balance before reweighting¶

    Variable Treated Never-treated Std. Diff
    n_total 90.6 9.8 +0.18
    share_pregao_eletronico 0.110 0.011 +0.55
    share_convite 0.142 0.203 −0.28
    share_dispensa 0.191 0.126 +0.33
    single_bidder_rate 0.163 0.164 −0.01

    Entropy balancing failed

    The optimization reached a degenerate solution: all control weights collapsed to zero. The never-treated municipalities are too fundamentally different from treated ones for entropy balancing to find valid weights. This confirms the comparison group problem.

    Implication¶

    The entropy balancing failure is itself informative — it proves the never-treated group is inadequate as a standalone counterfactual. The preferred specification is Spec D (not-yet-treated), where treated municipalities serve as their own controls before adoption. Results are robust across Spec A (never-treated), Spec D, and trimmed comparison groups.

    Summary¶

    Action Result For the paper
    1. Process DiD Null (p > 0.29 for all outcomes) Appendix: informative null
    2. Decreto 9.412 3/3 correct, p < 0.001 Main text: Figure 1
    3. Size heterogeneity Waterbed in Q1 (p = 0.089*) Main text: discussion
    4. Pilar 1 vs 2 Enforcement null vs Rules 7/8 Main text: core contrast
    5. Entropy balancing Failed (degenerate) Main text: transparency

    Bottom line for resubmission

    The process-level DiD (Action 1) does not produce the "clean causal effect" the referee wanted — NLL processes look identical to 8.666 processes on observables. But this is the finding: the NLL doesn't change process-level efficiency; it changes the composition of corruption at the institutional level. The Decreto 9.412 proof-of-concept (Action 2, p < 0.001), the sign test (7/8, p = 0.035), and the size heterogeneity (Action 3) together make the case that institutional rules reshape corruption form, not level.
